What is an Employer of Record (EOR) in Egypt?

An Employer of Record (EOR) in Egypt is a service that allows foreign companies to hire Egyptian employees without having a locally registered company. The EOR provider (Staffona) acts as the legal employer, taking on all responsibilities for payroll, tax withholding, social insurance, and labor law compliance. Meanwhile, the foreign company retains the right to manage the employee's work, goals, and daily activities.

This model is often referred to as "Employment Outsourcing" or "Professional Employer Organization (PEO)" in some contexts, although an EOR specifically takes on the full legal liability of employment. In Egypt, this is a highly effective way for international businesses to tap into the local talent market while remaining 100% compliant with local regulations.

The Legal Framework for EOR in Egypt

Employment in Egypt is primarily governed by Labor Law No. 12 of 2003. This comprehensive piece of legislation sets the standard for employment relationships across the country, covering everything from the recruitment process and initial onboarding to daily operations and eventual termination procedures. For a foreign company, complying with this law can be incredibly challenging due to the requirements for local physical representation, deep knowledge of Arabic legal terminology, and frequent interactions with various government bodies, including the Ministry of Labor and the Social Insurance Authority.

Our EOR service provides the necessary legal framework to navigate these complexities. We ensure that every employee has a valid, legally-vetted employment contract that meets all the requirements of the Egyptian Ministry of Labor. These contracts are meticulously drafted to include specific clauses regarding working hours, salary structures, job descriptions, and employee rights, ensuring that both the employer and the employee are protected. We also manage the mandatory registration of contracts with the local labor office, a step that is often overlooked by foreign companies but is essential for legal compliance.

Furthermore, the legal framework in Egypt is not static. Regular amendments and new circulars from the Ministry of Labor can change the requirements for minimum wage, insurance ceilings, and employee benefits. As your EOR partner, Staffona takes on the responsibility of monitoring these changes and implementing them immediately, ensuring that your business never falls out of compliance.

Egyptian Labor Law Reference

Key aspects of the Egyptian Labor Law that foreign employers must understand include:

  • Trial (Probation) Period: Maximum of 3 months. An employee cannot be placed on probation more than once with the same employer.
  • Working Hours: Maximum of 8 hours per day and 48 hours per week. Employees must have at least one full day of rest per week.
  • Overtime: Must be paid for any hours worked beyond the standard limit. Rates vary depending on whether the work is done during the day, at night, or on a public holiday.
  • Notice Period: Typically 2 months for employees with less than 10 years of service, and 3 months for those with more.

Social Insurance Compliance

In Egypt, social insurance is mandatory for all employees. It provides coverage for old age, disability, death, and work-related injuries. Under the Social Insurance and Pensions Law No. 148 of 2019, both the employer and the employee must contribute a percentage of the employee's "insurance salary" to the Social Insurance Authority.

Staffona handles the registration of every employee with the Social Insurance Authority, ensures that monthly contributions are calculated correctly, and manages the remittance process. This is one of the most critical aspects of compliance in Egypt, as failure to pay social insurance can lead to severe penalties and legal action.

Income Tax Obligations

Every employee in Egypt is subject to progressive income tax. As the EOR, Staffona is responsible for calculating the correct amount of tax to be withheld from each employee's salary and remitting it to the Egyptian Tax Authority on a monthly basis. We also handle the annual tax reconciliation process, ensuring that the total tax paid throughout the year matches the employee's final liability.

Employment Contract Compliance

Under Egyptian law, all employment contracts must be written in Arabic and three copies must be made (one for the employer, one for the employee, and one for the Social Insurance Office). While Staffona can provide bilingual contracts (Arabic/English), the Arabic version remains the legally binding version in the event of a dispute. We ensure that your contracts are not only compliant with the law but also protect your intellectual property and confidential information.

Risks of Non-Compliance

The consequences of ignoring local labor laws in Egypt can be significant:

  • Fines: Regulatory bodies frequently audit companies and can impose heavy fines for missed social insurance payments or incorrect tax filings.
  • Legal Disputes: Employees in Egypt are well-protected by the law, and labor courts often favor the employee in disputes where the employer has not followed proper legal procedures.
  • Reputational Risk: Non-compliance can damage your brand's reputation in the local market, making it harder to attract and retain top talent.
  • Operational Disruptions: Legal issues can lead to delays in operations and even the suspension of business activities.

Understanding Income Tax Brackets in Egypt

Egypt uses a progressive income tax system. Taxable income is calculated after deducting the personal exemption and the employee's share of social insurance. Social Insurance Contributions

Social insurance is a major component of payroll in Egypt. Both the employer and the employee must contribute to the Social Insurance Authority.

  • Employer Contribution: 18.75% of the insurance salary.
  • Employee Contribution: 11% of the insurance salary.

There is a minimum and maximum cap for the "insurance salary," which is adjusted annually in January. Calculating this correctly is essential, as the insurance salary may differ from the actual gross salary.

Monthly Payroll Reporting & Tax Filings

In the Egyptian regulatory environment, compliance is an ongoing process that extends far beyond the simple act of paying employees. The government requires a rigorous series of reports and filings to ensure that all social contributions and taxes are being handled correctly. Staffona takes full responsibility for these statutory requirements, providing you with a seamless experience.

  • Monthly Income Tax Filing: Every month, we calculate the total income tax withheld from your employees' salaries. This must be submitted to the Egyptian Tax Authority within the first 15 days of the following month. Our system ensures these payments are made precisely and on time, avoiding any potential interest charges.
  • Quarterly Salary Tax Returns (Form 4): Every quarter, employers must file a comprehensive return that summarizes all salaries paid, taxes withheld, and any exemptions applied. This is a critical document for the tax authority's auditing process, and we ensure it is perfectly reconciled with your monthly filings.
  • Social Insurance Monthly Report (Form 2): We manage the ongoing relationship with the Social Insurance Authority. This includes filing the monthly reports that detail any changes in your workforce, salary adjustments, and the corresponding contributions. This ensures that your employees' insurance coverage remains uninterrupted.
  • Annual Salary Tax Reconciliation: At the end of each fiscal year, a final reconciliation must be performed. This process compares the total tax withheld throughout the year against the employee's final annual liability, taking into account the full year's worth of exemptions and bracket movements. We handle this complex calculation and file the final settlement with the tax office.

By managing these filings, we protect your company from the administrative burden and legal risks associated with Egyptian tax bureaucracy. You receive copies of all filings for your corporate records, ensuring total transparency.

Common Payroll Compliance Mistakes in Egypt

Foreign companies often encounter issues when trying to manage Egyptian payroll internally:

  1. Incorrect Insurance Salary: Failing to adjust the insurance salary based on the annual caps or miscalculating the employer/employee shares.
  2. Missing Tax Deadlines: The Egyptian Tax Authority is strict with deadlines; late filings result in automatic interest and penalties.
  3. Inaccurate Benefit Valuation: Non-cash benefits or allowances must sometimes be included in the taxable income, a nuance that is often missed.
  4. Poor Record Keeping: Failure to maintain original signed payslips or proper labor files can lead to significant problems during a Ministry of Labor audit.

Common Contract Types in Egypt

Choosing the right contract type is essential for both compliance and flexibility. Under Egyptian Labor Law, there are two primary types:

Egypt Labor Law Explained for Foreign Employers

Updated: January 2026 â€¢ 15 min read

Navigating the Egyptian legal system is a prerequisite for any business looking to hire locally. The Egyptian Labor Law (No. 12 of 2003) is the primary statute governing private sector employment. This guide breaks down its most important provisions for international employers.

Key Principles of the Egyptian Labor Law

The law is designed to be protective of employees, reflecting its social and economic goals. It covers all private sector employees, whether they are Egyptian or foreign (provided they have a valid work permit).

1. Employment Contracts

Every employment relationship must be formalized with a written contract. According to Article 32, the contract must be in Arabic. If a second language is used, the Arabic version prevails in local courts.

  • Fixed-Term: Ends on a specific date. Can be renewed.
  • Indefinite: No end date. Provides more stability for the employee.
  • Probation: Must be explicitly stated and cannot exceed 3 months.

2. Working Hours and Rest Periods

Standard work hours are 8 hours per day, totaling 48 hours per week (Article 80). During Ramadan, many businesses choose to reduce working hours, although this is not a statutory requirement for the private sector.

  • Weekly Rest: At least 24 consecutive hours after every 6 days of work.
  • Overtime: Daytime overtime is paid at 135% of the basic wage, and nighttime overtime at 170%.

3. Annual and Sick Leave

Employees are entitled to annual paid leave once they complete 6 months of service.

  • Standard Leave: 21 days per year.
  • Extended Leave: 30 days for employees over 50 years old or with 10 years of service.
  • Sick Leave: Entitlement varies depending on the length of service and is partially covered by social insurance.

4. Termination of Employment

Terminating an employee in Egypt is a complex process. "At-will" employment does not exist. Termination must be for a "just cause" as defined in Articles 69 and 70.

  • Just Causes: Including gross negligence, consistent poor performance (after warnings), or disclosure of trade secrets.
  • Labor Office Role: Disputes are often referred to a specialized committee at the Ministry of Labor before going to court.

5. Social Insurance and Taxes

Registration with the Social Insurance Authority is mandatory from day one of employment. Employers who fail to register their staff face significant fines and potential criminal liability in extreme cases.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can we use a foreign law contract?

While you can have a "side agreement" under foreign law, the local relationship will always be governed by the mandatory provisions of the Egyptian Labor Law.

Are there special rules for female employees?

Yes, the law provides specific protections for female employees, including maternity leave (90 days) and nursing breaks.

Payroll Taxes in Egypt â€“ Employer Guide

Updated: January 2026 â€¢ 12 min read

Understanding your tax obligations is a critical part of managing a team in Egypt. This guide provides a detailed overview of the different types of payroll taxes that employers must handle.

1. Personal Income Tax (Salary Tax)

In Egypt, salary tax is withheld by the employer at the source. It is a progressive tax applied to the employee's total income from employment.

  • Tax Residency: Employees spending more than 183 days in Egypt are considered tax residents.
  • Taxable Base: Includes base salary, bonuses, commissions, and most allowances.
  • Exemptions: There is a mandatory personal exemption (9,000 EGP per year) and a tax-free first bracket.

2. Social Insurance Contributions

While often viewed as a "benefit," social insurance is a statutory tax-like contribution that is mandatory for all Egyptian employees.

  • Employer Share: 18.75%
  • Employee Share: 11%
  • Base: Calculated on the "insurance salary" which has a statutory minimum and maximum.

3. Other Mandatory Contributions

Employers are also responsible for several smaller funds that support social and national initiatives:

  • Martyrs Fund (Takaful): A small percentage (typically 0.05%) of the total payroll.
  • Emergency Subsidy Fund: 1% of the basic salary, designed to support workers during industrial crises.

4. Monthly and Quarterly Compliance

Employers must not only pay the taxes but also file the correct documentation:

  • Monthly: Payment of withheld taxes and social insurance.
  • Quarterly: Filing of Form 4 (Salary Tax Return).
  • Annually: Filing of the final tax reconciliation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are there penalties for late payment?

Yes, the tax authority applies interest on any late payments, which is currently based on the discount rate set by the Central Bank of Egypt + 2%.

Is the employer responsible for the employee's tax return?

For salary income, the employer handles the withholding and the annual reconciliation. The employee only needs to file a personal return if they have other sources of income.

Social Insurance in Egypt Explained

Updated: January 2026 â€¢ 10 min read

The social insurance system is the backbone of social security in Egypt. For employers, it is a mandatory legal obligation that requires monthly management and reporting.

Overview of the Social Insurance Law

The system is governed by Law No. 148 of 2019. It unified several previous funds into a single comprehensive system covering old-age pensions, disability, death, and work-related injuries.

Mandatory Contributions

Contributions are calculated as a percentage of the "subscription salary" (also known as the insurance salary).

  • Employer Share: 18.75% of the subscription salary.
  • Employee Share: 11% of the subscription salary.
  • Total Contribution: 29.75%

The Subscription Salary Caps

The subscription salary is not always equal to the employee's total gross salary. It is subject to a minimum and a maximum cap, which are increased by 15% every January.

In 2026, it is essential to ensure that your payroll system is updated with the latest caps to avoid underpayment or overpayment of contributions.

Benefits Provided to Employees

By contributing to the system, employees gain access to several key benefits:

  • Pensions: Monthly payments upon reaching the retirement age.
  • Disability & Death: Financial support for the employee or their family in the event of disability or death.
  • Health Insurance: Access to the national health insurance scheme.
  • Unemployment Insurance: Temporary financial support in case of job loss.

Employer Responsibilities

  1. Registration: Every new employee must be registered using Form 1 within the first week of employment.
  2. Monthly Payment: Contributions must be paid to the Social Insurance Authority by the 15th of the following month.
  3. Deregistration: When an employee leaves, they must be deregistered using Form 6 to stop the contribution obligation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are foreign employees covered?

Generally, the mandatory scheme is for Egyptian nationals. Foreigners are only covered if there is a reciprocal social security agreement between Egypt and their home country.

What happens if we don't pay?

Non-payment leads to automatic fines and interest. It can also block the company from obtaining necessary licenses or permits.

Termination Rules in Egypt â€“ Legal Guide

Updated: January 2026 â€¢ 12 min read

Terminating an employment relationship in Egypt is one of the most legally sensitive areas for any employer. This guide outlines the proper procedures and risks involved.

No "At-Will" Employment

It is important for foreign employers to understand that "at-will" employment, as practiced in the US, does not exist in Egypt. Termination must always be based on a valid legal reason, or it can be challenged as an "arbitrary dismissal."

Types of Termination

1. Termination of Fixed-Term Contracts

A fixed-term contract naturally expires on its end date. If the employer does not wish to renew, they should provide notice as per the contract terms. However, if the contract is renewed several times, it may be treated as an indefinite contract by a labor court.

2. Resignation

An employee can resign by giving the required notice period. The resignation must be in writing and is subject to the employee's right to withdraw it within one week.

3. Dismissal for Just Cause (Article 69)

An employer can dismiss an employee for serious misconduct, including:

  • Using a false identity or forged documents.
  • Committing an error that causes substantial financial loss to the employer (must be reported to authorities within 24 hours).
  • Repeated failure to follow safety instructions.
  • Absence without valid reason for more than 20 intermittent days or 10 consecutive days in a year (after written warnings).
  • Disclosure of trade secrets.

The Notice Period

For indefinite contracts, the mandatory notice period is:

  • 2 Months: For employees with less than 10 years of service.
  • 3 Months: For employees with 10 or more years of service.

Consequences of Arbitrary Dismissal

If a court finds a dismissal to be arbitrary, the employer may be ordered to pay compensation. The minimum compensation is usually two months of the full salary for each year of service, in addition to payment in lieu of notice and any accrued leave.

Best Practices for Risk Mitigation

  • Document Everything: Maintain a clear file of performance reviews, warnings, and correspondence.
  • Follow the Process: Always conduct an internal investigation before dismissing for misconduct.
  • Seek Legal Advice: Never terminate an employee in Egypt without consulting a local labor law expert.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can we pay a "buyout" instead of notice?

Yes, the employer can choose to pay the employee for the notice period and terminate the relationship immediately.

What is a Form 6?

Form 6 is the official document submitted to the Social Insurance Authority to signify the end of the employment relationship and stop the monthly contribution obligation.

Risks of Misclassifying Contractors in Egypt

Updated: January 2026 â€¢ 12 min read

Hiring full-time workers as "independent contractors" or "freelancers" is a common mistake made by foreign companies entering the Egyptian market. This article explores the legal and financial risks of misclassification.

What is Employee Misclassification?

Misclassification occurs when a company treats a worker as an independent contractor for tax and legal purposes, even though the reality of the working relationship is that of an employer and employee.

How Egyptian Authorities Define an Employee

The Egyptian Ministry of Labor and the Tax Authority look at the substance of the relationship, not just the title of the contract. Key indicators of employment include:

  • Control: The employer directs when, where, and how the work is done.
  • Exclusivity: The worker is required to work only for that company.
  • Tools & Equipment: The company provides the necessary tools (e.g., laptop, software).
  • Integration: The worker's role is a core part of the business operations.

The Risks of Misclassification

1. Back-Payment of Social Insurance

If a "contractor" is found to be an employee, the Social Insurance Authority can demand the back-payment of all employer and employee contributions, plus significant interest and late fees. This can amount to 30%+ of the total historical payments made to the worker.

2. Tax Liabilities and Penalties

The Tax Authority may claim unpaid salary taxes that should have been withheld. Again, this comes with automatic interest charges that can multiply the original debt.

3. Employment Rights Claims

A misclassified worker can file a claim for statutory benefits they missed out on, such as annual leave, end-of-service gratuity, and even compensation for arbitrary dismissal if the contract is terminated without following proper labor law procedures.

4. Reputation and Audit Risk

Once a company is flagged for misclassification, it is likely to be subject to more frequent audits by both the labor and tax authorities.

How to Protect Your Business

The safest way to hire in Egypt is to use a compliant Employer of Record (EOR) model. By hiring through Staffona, you ensure that every worker is correctly classified, registered with the proper authorities, and provided with all statutory benefits from day one.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can we use a global PEO contract?

A global contract is not enough. To be safe, the relationship must be governed by a local contract that is registered with the Egyptian Social Insurance office.

What if the worker prefers to be a freelancer?

The preferences of the worker do not override the statutory requirements. The company remains liable for the misclassification regardless of any agreement signed by the worker.
